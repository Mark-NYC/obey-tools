Love One Another
Jn 15:12 · Command 5 of 12
The Command
As I Have Loved You

Jesus doesn't simply command us to love — he sets the standard for what that love looks like. The benchmark is not how we feel or what is convenient. The standard is the cross. "As I have loved you" points to sacrificial, costly, laying-down-your-life love.

"My command is this: Love each other as I have loved you. Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends."

— John 15:12–13

This kind of love is not a feeling — it is a decision and an action. It reaches across barriers of race, class, religion, and status. It inconveniences itself. It notices who others overlook. Jesus said the world would recognize his followers by this love — not by their beliefs alone, but by how they treat people.

The Story
The Good Samaritan — Luke 10:25–37

A legal expert stood up to test Jesus. "What must I do to inherit eternal life?" Jesus asked him what the law said. The man answered correctly: love God and love your neighbor. But then he asked the question that revealed his true motivation:

"And who is my neighbor?"

— Luke 10:29

He wanted to define "neighbor" narrowly — to find the line beyond which he didn't have to love. Jesus responded with a story. A man was beaten, robbed, and left for dead on the road. A priest walked by on the other side. A Levite did the same. Then a Samaritan — despised and considered an outsider by Jews — came along.

But a Samaritan, as he traveled, came where the man was; and when he saw him, he took pity on him. He went to him and bandaged his wounds, pouring on oil and wine. Then he put the man on his own donkey, brought him to an inn and took care of him.

— Luke 10:33–34

The Samaritan didn't ask if the man deserved help. He didn't consider the risk. He saw a need and responded. Jesus then flipped the question: "Which of these three do you think was a neighbor to the man who fell into the hands of robbers?" The expert said, "The one who had mercy on him." Jesus replied: "Go and do likewise."

Reflect
Questions for Discussion
  • Who in your life do you find it hardest to love? What would it look like to love them "as Christ has loved you"?
  • The Samaritan was inconvenienced, took a risk, and spent his own money. What are you currently unwilling to sacrifice in order to love someone else?
  • The lawyer wanted to define "neighbor" narrowly. Are there groups of people you tend to exclude from your circle of concern?
  • Jesus says love is the mark of his disciples. Looking at your life this past week, would people recognize you as his follower by how you loved?
Obey
Take a Step

This week, identify one person you have been avoiding or overlooking. Take a practical, inconvenient step to love them — a meal, a conversation, a concrete act of help. Don't wait until you feel like it.

Ask yourself: Who is my Samaritan? — who is someone from a group you might naturally dismiss or avoid? Pray for them by name, and ask God to show you one way to cross the road toward them.
