The Command
This Is How You Should Pray

Jesus assumes his followers will pray — he says "when you pray," not "if you pray." He gives a model not as a script to recite but as a pattern to pray through: honoring God's name, seeking his kingdom, asking for daily provision, confessing sin, receiving and extending forgiveness, and asking for protection.

"This, then, is how you should pray: 'Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name, your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven. Give us today our daily bread. And forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors. And lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from the evil one.'"

— Matthew 6:9–13

Notice how the Lord's Prayer begins: "Our Father." Not "Almighty God" or "Creator." Father. Jesus is teaching his disciples that they have access — personal, familial, direct access — to the God of the universe. Prayer is not a religious performance. It is a conversation with your Father.

The Pattern
How to Pray Through It
Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name — Start with worship. Acknowledge who God is before you ask for anything.
Your kingdom come, your will be done — Surrender. Submit your agenda to his. Ask him to advance his kingdom in your life and circumstances.
Give us today our daily bread — Dependence. Bring your real, practical needs to God. Don't spiritualize — ask for what you need today.
Forgive us our debts — Confession. Be specific. Name what you've done and receive his forgiveness.
As we forgive our debtors — Release. Name anyone you need to forgive. Choose to let it go.
Lead us not into temptation — Honesty. Acknowledge where you are weak and ask for protection.
The Story
The Context — Matthew 6:5–15

Jesus gave this prayer in the middle of the Sermon on the Mount, sandwiched between two warnings about hypocritical religious practice. He warned against praying to be seen by others — standing in public places to perform your piety. He called them "hypocrites" because their audience was people, not God.

"But when you pray, go into your room, close the door and pray to your Father, who is unseen. Then your Father, who sees what is done in secret, will reward you."

— Matthew 6:6

He also warned against "babbling like pagans" who thought they would be heard for their many words. The point was not length or volume — God is not more likely to hear you if you pray longer or louder. He already knows what you need before you ask. Prayer is not informing God; it is communing with him.

"And when you pray, do not keep on babbling like pagans, for they think they will be heard because of their many words. Do not be like them, for your Father knows what you need before you ask him."

— Matthew 6:7–8

Reflect
Questions for Discussion
  • What does your current prayer life actually look like? Be honest — is it consistent, occasional, or almost nonexistent?
  • Jesus warns against praying to be seen. Are there ways your prayers are still more about performance than real communication with God?
  • Which part of the Lord's Prayer is hardest for you — worship, surrender, dependence, confession, or forgiveness?
  • God "knows what you need before you ask." If that's true, why does Jesus still tell us to ask?
Obey
Take a Step

Pray through the Lord's Prayer every day this week — not by reciting it, but by using each line as a prompt. Spend at least one minute on each section. Let it open up real, specific conversation with God.

Find a "prayer room" — it doesn't need to be a literal room. Pick a place, a time, and a posture for daily prayer this week and protect that time. Close your door. Put your phone away. Pray in secret.
